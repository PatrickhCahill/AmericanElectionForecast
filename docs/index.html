The system v. the People

The people's vote does not directly determine the winner of the presidential election. The winner is the person who wins the vote in a subset of states whose electoral votes sum to 270. In 2016, Hillary Clinton won the overall vote by running up the margins in California, New York and other safe Democratic states, but she lost where it counted - across the Rust Belt of America. The Electoral College Gap is the gap between the tipping point state and the popular vote - since Harris entered the race, it has been growing. So have the chances that Trump wins the electoral college and but loses the popular vote, again.

A graph inspired by 538.

It's possible to win the popular vote but lose the electoral vote. This plot shows a collection of 1000 simulations. The greyed out values show were one candidate won the electoral college but the other won the popular vote. The chance of this outcome occuring has grown since Kamala Harris entered the race.

The fivethirtyeight team publish their version of this graph which inspired me to make this. What's notable is that the tails of my distributions are much smaller. Some of their electoral outcomes have one candidate or the other winning winning by over a 20 point margin - my uncertainty bounds are smaller. This likely means my model predictions are more confident (read arrogant) than their's.
